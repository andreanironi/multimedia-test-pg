Horizon scanning

Horizon scanning

Horizon scanning is essential in an age when pests can travel from one side of the globe to the other in a matter of days. EFSA keeps track of the pests of tomorrow through regular monitoring of the media and scientific literature worldwide. In collaboration with the European Commission's Joint Research Centre (JRC) and EU Member States, EFSA screens scientific and other relevant publications and the media to identify new, emerging or recurring pests that might be of concern to the EU territory. A monthly newsletter highlights the most relevant news on plant pests worldwide. Any new or emerging non-regulated pests identified through the monitoring are evaluated according to the risk they pose, and the information is shared with the European Commission and Member States.

Pest surveillance

EU Member States must be prepared to meet the increasing threat posed by plant pests. To help them plan their pest surveys, EFSA provides a toolkit that includes pest survey cards, survey guidelines, and statistical tools. The aim is to assist national authorities in carrying out plant pest surveys in their territories and to harmonise surveillance methods across the EU.

Priority pests

Priority Pests

To help the EU and its Member States prepare their defence strategies, the European Commission has identified 20 priority pests that present the most serious economic, environmental and social threat to EU countries. The list is based on assessments carried out by the Commission's Joint Research Centre (JRC) and EFSA on pests such as Xylella fastidiosa, the Japanese beetle, the Asian long-horned beetle, citrus greening and citrus black spot. The methodology devised by the JRC and EFSA allows for the pests to be assessed quantitatively and ranked according to the severity of the threat they pose. The results show, for example, that the bacterium X. fastidiosa could cost the agricultural sector over €5.5 billion per year in lost production were it to spread across the entire EU. Similarly, should the Asian long-horned beetle (Anoplophora glabripennis) spread in the EU, the costs to the forestry sector could be as high as €50 billion.

High-risk plants

High-risk plants

As part of the new plant health rules, the Commission has drawn up a list of high-risk plants whose entry into the EU is prohibited, pending a commodity risk assessment. These are plants that are known hosts of particularly dangerous pests. EFSA is carrying out commodity risk assessments of the listed plants so that the Commission can decide whether they should remain prohibited or be de-listed. Prior to beginning this work in 2019, EFSA published a report detailing the information third countries need to provide when challenging prohibition of a plant or plant product, followed by guidance explaining the methodology EFSA uses when carrying out the assessments.

Pest risk assessments

Pest risk assessments

One of EFSA's key tasks is to assess whether plant pests should be considered for inclusion in the EU's overall lists of quarantine pests. It does this by conducting pest categorisations and/or pest risk assessments. A pest categorisation is the first phase of a risk assessment. It describes the pest's identity, biology, distribution, regulatory status, host range, and its ability to enter, establish and spread in the EU. The categorisation states whether the pest fulfils the criteria to be considered as a quarantine pest (absence or limited presence in the EU but with the ability to enter, establish and spread) or a regulated non-quarantine pest (already present in the EU). A pest that has the characteristics of a quarantine pest may undergo a quantitative pest risk assessment, which includes an evaluation of risk reduction options and current EU control measures.

Xylella fastidiosa

Xylella fastidiosa

One of EFSA's most high-profile pest risk assessments was the evaluation of Xylella fastidiosa, a vector-transmitted plant pathogen associated with serious diseases in a wide range of plants. The bacterium was detected for the first time in the EU on olive trees in Puglia, southern Italy, in October 2013. Since then it has also been reported in France, Spain, Portugal and in a greenhouse in Germany (the latter already declared as eradicated in 2018). Emergency control measures are in place across the EU. EFSA's assessment used computer modelling to simulate how X. fastidiosa spreads across short and long distances under different conditions. The modelling demonstrated the importance of controlling the insects that are known to transmit the pathogen in Europe, of effective surveillance, and of minimising the delay between detection and implementation of control measures.

International Year of Plant Health

International Year of Plant Health

Plants make up 80 per cent of the food we eat. Yet they are under constant attack from pests, with the FAO estimating that up to 40 percent of food crops are destroyed globally each year. That leaves millions of people hungry and seriously damages our agriculture and environment. Factors such as climate change, globalisation of trade and the tourism boom are making things worse, increasing the risk of new plant pests emerging and damaging crops, gardens and parks. That is why the United Nations has designated 2020 the International Year of Plant Health (IYPH): to raise awareness of how protecting plant health can help end hunger, reduce poverty, protect the environment, boost economic development, and contribute to achieving the UN's Millennium Development Goals.
